Poop Pills

Goal

Develop a pill version of Fecal Microbiota Transplant (FMT)

Who

OpenBiome

When

September 2014 - May 2015

Background

One of the most common hospital infections, Clostridium difficile, also known as “C. diff,” inflicts debilitating symptoms, affects over 500,000 Americans each year, and kills almost 30,000 of them.  When antibiotics don’t work, a Fecal Microbiota Transplant (FMT) can be used to send an army of good bacteria into your gut to crowd out the infection. Patients feel better within hours 90 percent of the time! FMT works in just the opposite way antibiotics do. Where antibiotics wipe out infection, FMT overflows the area, leaving the infectious bacteria with nowhere to hide. Until 2015, doctors would administer an FMT via a colonoscopy or endoscopy tube, an invasive but effective medical procedure.

Challenge

Fecal matter is not the easiest thing to encapsulate. It’s 70 percent water; when you put it into a gelatin capsule, a traditional shell for drugs, the capsule melts from the inside out.

Team

I researched, designed, and executed all experiments. I worked with two microbiology PhDs for scientific support, a physician for clinical oversight, and a manufacturing team to bring the product to life at scale.

Solution

My solution was to create small “fences” around bubbles of poop by mixing a hydrophobic (water-hating) liquid with the hydrophilic (water-loving) active ingredient (the poop).  In real life that means, I created a poop pill: shelf-stable, orally available dosage form of FMT delivered by Microbial Emulsion Matrix Technology (MEM Tech).

Impact

Over 250k pills have been made using my method. To date, the pill I designed has treated over 2,000 patients with C. diff and has been used to study the effects of FMT in 25+ clinical trials including inflammatory bowel disease (IBD), immuno-oncology (I/O), and primary sclerosing cholangitis (PSC).

Process

Follow the photos below for descriptions of process, prototypes, and poop geysers. There were a few more ups and downs, many more protoypes, and infinitely more failures not recorded in the following diagrams.

Concept Doodling

I started my assignment with a concept drawing.

First Idea Coating

I knew that the water content of poop would melt the capsule from the inside out. So, I considered coating the outside of the capsule to keep the water in.

Hammering Out Prototypes

While most coating processes are completed at a large scale, I started by making a small peg board to test different coatings.

Each capsule half rested on top of a nail while I poured the coatings over the top. This seemed promising until these capsules were stuck to the peg board causing the coating to flake off.

Outsourcing a Better Coat

After a few hours, the professionally coated capsules started to pucker leading us to ponder what additional materials I could use to stablize the coating and capsule.

Adding Stregnth

I experimented with starches to fortify the coating. However, many of the added excipients did not react well to moisture or were too dry and increased flaking and cracking.

Mixing up MEM Tech

After many more failed prototypes and brainstorming sessions, Microbial Emulsion Matrix Technology was born!

Emulsions are mixtures of water and oil. I tested oils with varying melting and freezing points to find an oil combination that stayed solid at room temperature to immobilize the water droplets but liquid at body temperature to release the bubbles of microbiota after ingested by the patient.

Stability Success

After a poop-ton of failures and bad ideas, MEM Tech was a success. It proved to be stable at room temperature as well as resilent in frozen storage. And most importantly, the process seemed to be scalable. Shown is one of the first successful test batches.

Scaling Improvements

To ensure that the pills could be made at a reasonable scale, I tested the reproducibility of the recipe in larger and larger batches and worked with manufacturing technicians to smooth out handling and operating differences.

Training the Team

I wrote standard operating procedures and training work instructions and oversaw one-on-one training of each manufacturing facturing technician.

Patenting & Filing a Biologics Master FIle

Alongside continuing to optimize the manufacturing process, my boss and I filed a a biologics master file to the FDA and wrote a patent for MEM Tech.

Shortly after training the manufacturing team, I had the honor of producing the first capsules intented to treat a patient.

First Capsules for Patient Treatment
Launching Capsule G3

Outside of the lab, I sprearheaded the product launch by coordinating the efforts of the external affairs, sales, and manufacturing teams. I made sure that the sales team was prepared to answer questions from clinicians and that the manufacturing team had enough time and equipment to meet inventory demand.

Impacting the Frontier of Microbiome Research

After more than 2,000 patients have been treated for C. diff with these poop pills, this product’s impact has reached new patient communities. Each new clinical trial catalyzes microbiome research and deepens our understanding of how bacteria affects our health.

Lessons

Be prepared to change your approach for different parts of the process. Development, scaling, and operations rely on different skills. Pausing to acknowledge that you’re shifting your “skill gears” is worth-while.
